Gregorian Calendar Months

Sr Month Days Note
1 January 31 Named after Janus, Roman god of beginnings; New Year's Day on January 1
2 February 28 (29 in leap years) Shortest month; Leap year every 4 years (e.g., 2024, 2028)
3 March 31 Named after Mars, Roman god of war; Spring begins (Northern Hemisphere)
4 April 30 Possibly from Latin "aperire" (to open, for budding flowers); Easter often occurs
5 May 31 Named after Maia, Roman goddess of growth
6 June 30 Named after Juno, Roman goddess of marriage; Summer begins (Northern Hemisphere)
7 July 31 Named after Julius Caesar; renamed from Quintilis
8 August 31 Named after Emperor Augustus; renamed from Sextilis
9 September 30 From Latin "septem" (seven), as it was the 7th month in the Roman calendar
10 October 31 From Latin "octo" (eight), as it was the 8th month in the Roman calendar
11 November 30 From Latin "novem" (nine); Thanksgiving in some countries
12 December 31 From Latin "decem" (ten); Christmas on December 25

Notes:

  • The Gregorian calendar is a solar calendar with 12 months and 365 days (366 in a leap year).
  • It was introduced by Pope Gregory XIII in 1582 to reform the Julian calendar.
  • Key Christian events like Christmas (December) and Easter (varies, often in March or April) are tied to this calendar.
  • Leap years occur every 4 years, except for century years not divisible by 400 (e.g., 1700, 1800, 1900 were not leap years, but 2000 was).

Christian Festivals

Christmas

Celebrates the birth of Jesus Christ. Observed with church services, gift-giving, carol singing, and festive meals. Traditions include decorating Christmas trees, exchanging presents, and attending midnight Mass.

Festival FAQs

Easter celebrates the resurrection of Jesus Christ from the dead, which is a foundational belief in Christianity. It symbolizes hope, renewal, and victory over sin and death. It is considered the most important Christian festival.

Christmas is celebrated on December 25th to commemorate the birth of Jesus Christ. While the exact date of His birth is not known, December 25th was chosen in the 4th century and aligns with earlier Roman festivals, symbolizing the coming of the "Light of the World."
